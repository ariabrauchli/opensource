Charlie's Weekly Blog

Throughout this capstone project, I took lead responsibility for the entire physical design and fabrication process of our custom bed system, from early CAD work to the final milled prototype. My work spanned parametric modeling, CNC machining, prototyping, testing, and final media production — forming the backbone of the project's technical development.

CAD & Parametric Design

I created the full 3D model of our transforming van bed system using Onshape, taking advantage of its collaborative tools and parametric design capabilities. Parametric modeling involves using variables to control features like bed dimensions and part thickness, so the model updates responsively when values change. This makes it highly adaptable across different vehicle platforms and user preferences.

CNC Machining & Fabrication

While modeling was done in Onshape, I transitioned to Fusion 360 to handle CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacturing) and toolpathing for CNC milling. I learned the entire CAM workflow, including machine-specific constraints like tool size and material allowances, and milled the physical prototype from Baltic birch plywood. I also performed all physical prototyping, from laser-cut models to full-scale CNC milled assemblies.

Iteration, Testing & Ergonomics

The project included continuous iterations based on hands-on testing and expert feedback. I assembled multiple prototypes, refining tolerances and joint designs, and researched ergonomics to improve user comfort and functionality. I also conducted expert interviews with van conversion industry professionals, which guided improvements in design for manufacturability and ease of use.

Team Support & Coordination

In addition to my own work, I coordinated with teammates in later phases to support their roles — including cushion production (Sammy) and web development (Aria). I shared technical guidance and helped ensure our contributions aligned for final integration.

Media & Final Presentation

I planned and filmed drone footage of the final product and led the editing of the final video, showcasing both the functionality of the bed system and the process behind its development.

Week-by-Week Progress Breakdown

Week 2

I focused on getting fully up to speed with Fusion 360 by completing a Skillshare tutorial. While I had previous experience with Onshape and SolidWorks, Fusion offered a new workflow for CAM and CNC-specific design. I found the interface intuitive and felt confident in my ability to use it for upcoming fabrication tasks. However, I didn't complete our reach goal of recreating the bed in Fusion, due to time constraints. Still, I gained the necessary foundation to move quickly in the following weeks.

Week 3

I completed the first parametric version of the bed model in Onshape, using global variables to define key dimensions such as bed length, thickness, and slat spacing. This made the design responsive and easily adaptable to different van configurations. I also conducted a one-on-one interview with our mentor to validate the parametric flexibility and discuss structural and ergonomic considerations. We identified issues that could arise during CNC translation and real-world use, such as instability at larger sizes and the importance of smooth sliding and folding mechanisms. These insights highlighted the need for physical prototyping and usability testing moving forward.

Week 4

This week was a huge leap forward in fabrication. I learned how to create toolpaths in Fusion 360's CAM environment, designed specifically for CNC milling. With support from our mentor Jeff, I completed the full workflow for using the CNC machine. We cut and assembled the first full-scale prototype, which revealed key challenges around tolerances and alignment. Understanding the physical constraints of our CNC machine and the specific bits it uses helped me refine the digital model for better compatibility with real-world fabrication.

Week 5

Building on feedback from our initial prototype, I iterated on the CAD design to improve glue joint alignment, address tolerance issues, and add features like adjustable backrest props. I spent a significant amount of time refining part geometry and ensuring the parametric structure remained stable. I also laser cut a scaled version of the updated model to verify improvements before milling again. In parallel, I participated in business plan development and worked with Sammy on our PSO statement, helping shape our approach for potential commercialization.

Week 6

I conducted our first expert interview with Cam Mahoney, a veteran van builder, and received actionable feedback on our design's marketability, safety, and usability. Cam emphasized the need for the bed system to be at least 90–100% complete before testing user assembly, suggesting that our next phase involve someone attempting to follow instructions to build it themselves. He also recommended modular mounting options like T-tracks and explained how commercial builds often face DOT compliance issues. Though I was distracted by flying to Minnesota to pick up my own van (a personal milestone!), I still made strong progress and gathered valuable insight.

Week 7

I met with our second expert, Mark Woodard, at Geotrek's warehouse and demoed our Onshape model. He gave helpful feedback on usability, including the idea of offering simplified presets (e.g., Small, Medium, Large) to reduce user overwhelm during customization. We also explored material options and mounting strategies. I finalized decisions around wood type and thickness, and scheduled our next milling session. Although I was excited by progress, I also began to feel the strain of being the primary technical lead, especially as team motivation seemed to dip.

Week 8

Although we had to postpone milling our second prototype due to a late-discovered design issue, I made significant progress finalizing the parametric model and generating all toolpaths. I purchased Baltic birch for the next version and prepared for cutting. I also launched a post on Facebook Marketplace offering "Custom CNC beds cut to order," which led to some initial interest — possibly our first customer. Additionally, I got external validation through forum discussions and even a job offer to design van furniture. These moments boosted my confidence and affirmed the project's real-world potential. Team communication improved this week too.

Week 9

This week marked the final push toward project completion. I successfully milled the final version of the bed on the CNC machine using our refined Fusion toolpaths and high-quality Baltic birch. Assembly went smoothly thanks to earlier adjustments in the parametric model, which minimized alignment and tolerance issues. The final build showcased the culmination of all prior design iterations and prototyping work. Additionally, I updated our Facebook Marketplace listing to feature photos and video of the finished product, which significantly improved its presentation and potential appeal to real-world customers, and I received a ton of interest from customers.

Weeks 10 & 11

Though I didn't formally log these weeks, my efforts focused on coordination, support, and final media production. I guided Sammy through finalizing the cushions to fit our milled design and helped Aria with content integration for the website. I wrapped up video editing, making sure our final deliverables clearly communicated the functionality and flexibility of the system. These weeks were less about new technical challenges and more about ensuring our presentation matched the quality of our build.
